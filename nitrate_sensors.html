Nitrate hysteresis and flushing indices in tile-drained watersheds

Quantifying sensitivity to sampling intervals

Preliminary Results - Subject to change pending final review.

Lead Author: Rudko, N.

Affiliation: Purdue University

Published In: Journal of American Water Resources Association (expected)

Year: 2026

Project Abstract

Storm event-based metrics, such as hysteresis (HI) and flushing (FI), are commonly used to differentiate nitrate pathways and sources in watersheds. Estimations of these event-based metrics typically use high frequency (15-minute – hourly) measurements, but daily data are also used due to their greater availability. To date, there has been no study quantifying how the accuracy of HI and FI might change as a function of sampling interval at the event scale, which could skew interpretation of potential nutrient pathways and sources. We used continuous measurements of nitrate collected at 9 tile-drained watersheds throughout the Midwest for 448 storm events. HI and FI were estimated from resampled time series at different sampling frequencies (1,2, 4, 8, 16, and 24 hours). The impact of higher interval measurements on HI and FI, compared to the reference 15-min data, was quantified using the Nash-Sutcliffe efficiency (NSE) and range of possible values for NSE were estimated through Monte Carlo simulations and bootstrapping. HI and FI were both similarly affected by sampling interval, with predictions of HI and FI both decreasing to unacceptable levels (NSE <0.65) at sampling intervals > 8 hours. And at 8-hour sampling intervals, the percentage of storms within the same quadrant when plotting HI and FI in bivariate space ranged from 59 – 94% (median = 84%). No apparent correlation existed between these metrics and watershed characteristics, such as drainage area and land-use. Correlations between HI and FI and hydroclimatic variables were similar when using daily or 15-minute values, suggesting that daily measurements can still be used to make inferences on watershed processes controlling nitrate transport in agricultural watersheds in the Midwest. Our results also demonstrate that caution should be used when interpreting outcomes using longer sampling intervals to estimate HI and FI.

Key Findings

  • 15 min publicly available nitrate data were used to estimate Hysteresis index (HI) and flushing index (FI) for 448 storm events at different sampling frequencies
  • Nitrate dynamics were captured for 90% of storm events at sampling intervals < 8 hours
  • Watersheds with greater tile drainage had more pronounced dilution patterns
  • Results provide guidance on suitable sampling intervals to estimate HI and FI
