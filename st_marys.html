Water Quality Sampling in Agricultural Watersheds

Investigating Nutrient Sources and Pathways in the Midwestern USA

Lead Author: Rudko, N.

Affiliation: Purdue University (PhD Research)

Published In: J. Nat. Resour. Agric. Ecosyst.

Year: 2024

Project Abstract

This study addressed the challenge of non-point source pollution in agricultural regions by utilizing high-frequency water quality monitoring alongside comprehensive geospatial data. We specifically investigated the St. Maryâ€™s River watershed, a tile-drained region in the Midwest, to quantify the dynamic transport of nitrate-nitrogen ($\text{NO}_3\text{-N}$) and dissolved reactive phosphorus ($\text{DRP}$). The research demonstrated that peak nutrient loads were significantly correlated with specific hydro-climatic events, such as large spring snowmelt and early summer storms. Furthermore, by isolating the different transport pathways (surface runoff vs. tile drainage), the research provided site-specific recommendations for Best Management Practices (BMPs), suggesting that edge-of-field practices like saturated buffers are necessary to complement in-field conservation practices.

Key Findings

  • **Nitrate Flush:** Observed a strong positive relationship between storm event magnitude and $\text{NO}_3\text{-N}$ concentrations, indicating a "flushing" mechanism driven by rapid tile flow.
  • **Phosphorus Source:** $\text{DRP}$ loads were primarily associated with surface runoff during extreme rainfall events, highlighting the importance of soil erosion control in these periods.
  • **Modeling Implication:** The data suggests that existing hydrological models (like SWAT) need finer-scale calibration of tile drainage parameters to accurately capture the timing and magnitude of nutrient delivery.
